Wind of change in Britain’s imports of goods

08/09/2022


On 21 July 2022, a tranche of new trade sanctions against Russia came into force. This mainly affected the energy market, and represented total independence from Russian energy for the first time, according to the data from Office for National Statistics.


The sanctions on Russia are a double-edged sword. On the one hand, western sanctions have jeopardised up to 40 per cent of Russia’s GDP, according to a study by Yale University, while on the other hand, many British people have felt the absence of Russian energy imports, as the UK’s inflation rate hits double digits in July.







The Bank of England blames the higher energy prices due to Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine, as one of the main reasons why the inflation rate is so high.


Last year the United Kingdom imported £10.3bn of goods from Russia, which accounted for 2.2% of all imports, making Russia its 12th biggest importing partner.


The UK’s biggest importing partner last year was China, the world’s second-largest economy.







As the tension between mainland China and Taiwan simmers, the chance of losing the ‘13.4%’ is rising, since British politicians seem to have no intention to defuse the situation.


British press reports suggest that some British MPs are planning to visit Taiwan later this year, following the Asia tour of US House Speaker Nancy Pelosi.


Meanwhile, in recent months, Liz Truss, the new PM, has urged western countries to ensure Taiwan can protect itself from China. In June, she remarked in an interview argued that the UK should provide Taiwan with weapons.


Liz Truss has shown her strong position against China.

Those actions and words no doubt have stepped on the red line set by the Chinese government, adding uncertainty to the future relationship between the United Kingdom and China. If China decided to use force in Taiwan, would British politicians act as tough as they talk?


Unlike trade embargos against Russia, a full block of importing Chinese goods would certainly do more damage to the British economy than China’s.


The math is quite simple here.


First of all, the UK has more relied on Chinese products and has imported more from China in the past three years (although this period also saw a ban on Chinese telecoms giant Huawei from the UK’s 5G network).







Although the UK’s total imports shrank by about £47bn in 2020, compared to the year before, the value of imports from China still increased by £8.4bn.


And more importantly, the £77bn worth of goods sold to the UK, which made about 2.58% of China’s total exports in 2021, are less crucial for China.
